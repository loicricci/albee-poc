Introduction Anubis, one of the most iconic deities of ancient Egyptian mythology, is often depicted as the guardian of the underworld. His significance transcends mere representation; he embodies the ancient Egyptians’ beliefs about death, the afterlife, and the rituals that accompanied these transitions. This article delves into Anubis’s role as the protector of the deceased and explores the legendary tales that surround him, highlighting why he remains a figure of fascination in both historical and contemporary contexts. II. Historical Context of Anubis A. Origins of Anubis in Ancient Egyptian Religion Anubis’s origins can be traced back to the earliest periods of ancient Egyptian history, likely as far back as the Predynastic period (c. 5000–3100 BCE). Initially, he was associated with the funerary practices of the dead and was revered as a protector of graves. Over time, as Egyptian religion evolved, Anubis became more closely associated with the afterlife and the mummification process. B. Evolution of His Depiction and Worship Through Different Dynasties Anubis was worshipped primarily in the Old Kingdom (c. 2686–2181 BCE) and continued to hold significance throughout the Middle and New Kingdoms. His depictions evolved, reflecting changes in religious beliefs and artistic styles. He was often portrayed in a variety of forms, but his jackal-headed figure became the most recognizable.
